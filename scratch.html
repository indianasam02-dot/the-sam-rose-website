The "Hook" of Fly Fishing

Why Fly Fishing?

Fly fishing is one part sport, one part science, and one part meditation. Unlike conventional fishing, the fly itself weighs almost nothing — you cast the weight of the line, relying on timing and technique rather than brute force. Western rivers like the Green, the Provo, and the Madison have shaped generations of anglers who learned to slow down, read current seams, and think like a trout. That patience is half the point. The quiet thrill of finally catching an elusive trout is the other.


How to Fly Fish: Step by Step

Fly fishing can be as complicated as you want it, or as simple as you want it. Here are the simple steps to get started and get your foot in the door to catching your first fish.

  1. Choose the Right Gear
    • Rod: a 9 ft, 5-weight is the ideal all-around starter rod
    • Reel: match your reel to your rod weight; for trout a basic drag system is plenty
    • Line: Weight-Forward Floating (WF5F) line casts easily and works with most fly types
    • Leader & Tippet: the leader tapers from thick to thin, connecting fly line to fly; use 4X–6X tippet for trout
    • Flies: start with a small selection of dry flies, nymphs, and a streamer
  2. Rig Up Your Setup
    • Thread the fly line through all rod guides from butt to tip
    • Connect the leader to the fly line using the loop-to-loop connection
    • Attach 12–18 inches of tippet to the leader using a double surgeon's knot
    • Tie your fly to the tippet using an improved clinch knot
  3. Learn to Read the Water
    • Seams: where fast water meets slow water; trout park just inside the slow side
    • Pools: deep slow sections behind bends — great for larger fish in morning and evening
    • Riffles: shallow choppy water over gravel; excellent feeding zones for aggressive trout
    • Structure: boulders, logs, undercut banks all break current and create resting lanes
    • Shade: trout feel exposed in bright sun; shaded banks are almost always productive
  4. Master the Overhead Cast
    • Back Cast: lift the rod from 9 o'clock to 1 o'clock in a smooth accelerating motion; stop crisply at 1
    • Pause: wait for the line to fully straighten behind you before starting the forward cast
    • Forward Cast: drive from 1 o'clock to 10 o'clock, stop sharply; the line will unroll and lay down
    • Common mistake: dropping your elbow on the back cast causes a wide loopy stroke — keep it close to your body
  5. Present the Fly Naturally
    • Cast upstream of your target and let the fly drift down on a slack line
    • Reach cast: extend your rod tip upstream as the fly lands to extend the drag-free drift
    • Mending: flip extra line upstream with your rod tip after the fly lands to counteract drag
    • Aim to land the fly 2–4 feet upstream of where the fish is holding
  6. Detect the Strike and Set the Hook
    • Dry fly strike: when the fly disappears in a rise, pause briefly then lift the rod tip firmly upstream
    • Nymph strike: the moment your indicator twitches or dips, lift immediately
    • Strip set for streamers: pull the line sharply with your line hand while keeping the rod low
    • Common mistake: setting too hard snaps light tippet — firm but controlled pressure only
    • Keep tension after the hook is set — slack line is how fish get off
  7. Fight and Land the Fish
    • Keep the rod tip high and bent — it acts as a shock absorber and protects your tippet
    • Apply side pressure to steer the fish out of fast water and into a calmer eddy
    • Retrieve line by stripping it with your line hand or reel it in when the fish calms
    • Never force a fish directly toward you — let it tire, then guide it to your net
    • Net the fish headfirst; a fish netted tail-first almost always thrashes free
  8. Practice Catch and Release
    • Wet your hands before handling a fish — dry hands strip the protective slime coat
    • Barbless hooks make release faster and less traumatic
    • Minimize air time — take your photo quickly and keep the fish near the water
    • Hold the fish upright in the current, facing upstream, until it kicks free on its own
    • In warm water above 65 degrees, trout are already stressed — give them extra recovery time
    • Leave the water and banks better than you found them
